Patterns in the Night

How prostitution in San Francisco has shifted across streets, hours, and decades

Assignment 2 for 02806 Social Data Analysis and Visualization, Spring 2026 by Emilie Vad Johannsen, Emil Bagge and Emma Astely

In San Fransico, USA, prostitution is illegal. But did you know, that before 2023 the police in San Fransisco (SF) could also arrest people in the street for the act of loitering? (Local News Matters, 2023, para. 2). This law intended to get sex workers off the street since “loitering” in a public space with the “intent to commit prostitution" was criminalized (Local News Matters, 2023, para. 2). In January 2023 the loitering was decriminalized, backed up by sex workers and other minority groups, arguing that the police was enforcing discriminatory behavior (Local News Matters, 2023, para. 2). Around the same time loitering was decriminalized, San Fransico Police Department (SFPD) installed barricades on the Capp Street corridor (a residential area in SF, experiencing a high number of sex workers), intended to nagivate sex workers and clients away from the area. A question then arises - has the installation of barricades removed the sex workers from the streets or have they gone to other neighborhoods to earn their money?

In order to answer the question raised above we have investigated data from SFPD from 2003 - 2025. The data covers police reports of different incident categories. Two datasets have been merged (data from 2003-2018 and 2018-2025), cleaned up and eight focus incident categories have been chosen. The chosen focus categories are assault, burglary, drug/alcohol offense, larceny theft, prostitution, robbery, vehicle theft and warrant. The two datasets, that has been merged into one, can be found here: (dataset_2003_to_2018). and (dataset_2018_to_2025).

Figure 2: The area chart shows the distribution of the eight displayed focus crimes throughout a 24-hour time period. The data covers the years 2003–2025. After 2023, which was the year when loitering was decriminalized and barriers were installed on the Capp Street corridor, there seems to be a rise in prostitution after 20:00 o'clock compared to the previous years. It is noticeable that the number of police report on prostitution has shifted from the period between 00:00–05:00 towards the time between 20:00–00:00 after 2023. The reasons for the temporal displacement can be many, but an assumption is that it could be connected to the decriminalization of loitering. If the sex workers no longer fear of being arrested for loitering, they may not be forced to work on the streets in the middle of the night, when it might be easier to hide. Another interesting tendency to point out is how prostitution follows the patterns of robbery, vehicle theft, and warrant, which all seem to spike between 20:00–00:00 after the year 2023. This could potentially mean that prostitution is moving into areas where the concentration of robbery, vehicle theft, and warrant is high.

Figure 3: The vizualisation shows each point for an arrest for prostitution as the colored points. There are primarily two areas appearing to be hotspot for this type of crime over the years, The Mission District, with Capp street marked with a red line and the red light district around Tenderloin. Zoom out to see a more general picture of crime in the city or zoom in to see a specific street or corner. Zooming in on Capp street, where the barriers were placed, we see a a high number of incidents on this street and neighboring streets and after 2022 more activity on the neighboring streets.

Zooming in on the map of police incident reports around the Mission District, we can see that the hotspots sort of “dance” around slightly across the years, while still remaining concentrated around the Mission District.

From around 2010, the hotspot appears less and less intense, which could indicate a decline in incidents of prostitution. However, this could also be a sign that parts of sex work moved to an online arena. This changed with the federal Fight Online Sex Trafficking Act in 2018, also known as FOSTA, which banned the posting of ads for sex work on the internet. The years around the Covid-19 pandemic meant less life on the streets in general, which could help explain the decline in incidents. However, moving into the years 2022 and 2023, there is a clearer shift. Did barriers on Capp Street and the “Dear John” letters prove effective at reducing incidents of prostitution?

That said, the data from the last two years, 2024 and 2025, shows that there is still a hotspot around the Mission District. Although no incidents appear to have happened directly on the well-known street, the incidents seem to have moved to other surrounding streets in the area. Although the data is still fresh, and it would be helpful to look at more years following the policing changes after 2022, we can already see a pattern: even with law and enforcement changes, the problem does not disappear, it simply shifts location. The barriers on Capp Street may not have removed the issue, but instead moved it to other streets within the Mission District.

So to answer the question has the installation of barricades removed the sex workers from the streets or have they gone to other neighborhoods to earn their money? we can only see the beginning of a pattern where the trade is pushed onto other streets in the neighborhood meaning that harder restrictions might not be the answer.
