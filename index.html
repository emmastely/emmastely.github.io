Patterns in the Night

How prostitution in San Francisco has shifted across streets, hours, and decades

Assignment 2 for 02806 Social Data Analysis and Visualization, Spring 2026 by Emilie Vad Johannsen (s200866), Emil Bagge (s204232) and Emma Astely (s223965)

In San Fransisco, USA, prostitution is illegal. But did you know, that before 2023 the police in San Fransisco (SF) could also arrest people in the street for the act of loitering? (Local News Matters, 2023). This law intended to get sex workers off the street since “loitering” in a public space with the “intent to commit prostitution" was criminalized (Local News Matters, 2023). In January 2023 the loitering was decriminalized, backed up by sex workers and other minority groups, arguing that the police was enforcing discriminatory behavior (Local News Matters, 2023). The same year loitering was decriminalized, San Fransisco Police Department (SFPD) installed barricades on the Capp Street corridor (Mission Local, 2023) (a residential area in SF, experiencing a high number of sex workers), intended to nagivate sex workers and clients away from the street. A question then arises - has the changed policy regarding prostitution in San Fransisco removed the sex workers from the streets or have they gone elsewhere to earn their money?

In order to answer the question raised above we have investigated data from SFPD from 2003 - 2025. The data covers police reports of different incident categories. Two datasets have been merged (data from 2003-2018 and 2018-2025), cleaned up and eight focus incident categories have been chosen. The chosen focus categories are assault, burglary, drug/alcohol offense, larceny theft, prostitution, robbery, vehicle theft and warrant. The two datasets, that has been merged into one, can be found here: (dataset_2003_to_2018). and (dataset_2018_to_2025).

Before we can answer whether decriminalization of loitering and installing barriers on the Capp Street corridor simply displaced sex workers, we must look at the historical context. Figure 1 maps out the total reported incidents of prostitution in SF from 2003 to 2025, revealing a drastic, decades-long decline that completely reshaped the city's baseline long before the events of 2023.

Figure 1: The figure above shows the number of incident reports for prostitution in San Fransisco as points. The static chart above, generated using Python and Matplotlib, visualizes the trend from 2003 to 2025. A clear shift in reported prostitution cases emerges over time and a sharp decline is seen from the year 2007 and onwards and another drop around 2020. It is important to note that the numbers don't reveal the total number of incidents of prostitution but rather the number of reported incidents.

The chart reveals a massive overall decline in reported incidents over the 22-year period. Reports peaked at over 1,400 cases in 2007, reflecting an era when police relied heavily on traditional "street sweeps." Following this peak, incidents plummeted throughout the 2010s as the trade largely migrated from physical street corners to digital platforms, making it far less visible to patrolling officers. (SFGATE, 2018).

The downward trend hit an absolute historical low around 2022, effectively flatlining near zero. This dramatic drop makes perfect sense when looking at the timeline: it aligns directly with the COVID-19 pandemic clearing the streets, immediately followed by the 2023 decriminalization of loitering introduced earlier. Without the legal power to arrest individuals simply for standing on the street with "intent," a massive portion of these police reports simply vanished. But while the overall volume has dropped drastically, looking closer at when these remaining incidents occur tells another interesting story.

Temporal data shows an interesting shift in the time of reported incidents after 2023. As earlier mentioned, 2023 was there year when loitering was decriminalized and barriers were installed on the Capp Street corridor. After 2023 the data shows a rise in prostitution between 20:00 and 00:00 o'clock compared to the previous years. It is noticeable that the number of police reports on prostitution has shifted from the period between 00:00–05:00 towards to the time between 20:00–00:00 after 2023. The reasons for the temporal displacement can be many, but an assumption is that the shift could be connected to the decriminalization of loitering. If the sex workers no longer fear for being arrested for loitering, they may not be forced to work on the streets in the middle of the night, when it might be easier to hide. Another interesting tendency to point out is how prostitution follows the patterns of robbery, vehicle theft, and warrant, which all seem to spike between 20:00–00:00 after the year 2023. This could potentially mean that prostitution is still happening in areas where the concentration of robbery, vehicle theft, and warrant is high.

Figure 2: The area chart shows the normalized distribution of the eight displayed focus crimes throughout a 24-hour time period. The data covers the years 2003–2025.

As the temporal distribution shows a change where reported incidents are pushed earlier in the day. This leads the question whether the change then also occurs where this activity happens. Has the so-called crackdown on prostitution driven away prostitution from the Mission District as policies like the decriminalization of loitering and the barriers could influence both the time and location of such activities. This leads us to explore how the spatial distribution of prostitution incidents has changed as the policy has changed between 2003 and 2025 to see if intervention has helped or simply moved it elsewhere.

Figure 3: The vizualisation shows each point for an arrest for prostitution as the colored points. There are primarily two areas appearing to be hotspot for this type of crime over the years, The Mission District, with Capp street marked with a red line and the red light district around Tenderloin. Zoom out to see a more general picture of crime in the city or zoom in to see a specific street or corner. Zooming in on Capp street, where the barriers were placed, we see a a high number of incidents on this street and neighboring streets and after 2022 more activity on the neighboring streets.

While the numbers of total incident reports over the years above showed an overall decline in incidents, the spatial distribution gives us a little bit more nuance. Rather than disappearing entirely from the Mission district, the hotspot remains and we can see that the they appear to shift around but ultimately persist within around the same area. This suggests that even as the total number of reports decreases both with the arena being moved increasingly online and with the exception of the Covid years, the activity itself may persist in ceertain areas or districts. However, moving into the years 2022 and 2023, the map appears almost empty, which raises an important question: Did barriers on Capp Street and the “Dear John” letters prove effective at reducing incidents of prostitution?

That said, the data from the last two years, 2024 and 2025, shows that there is still a hotspot around the Mission District. Although no incidents appear to have happened directly on the well-known Capp Street, the incidents seem to have moved to other surrounding streets in the area. Although the data is still fresh, and it would be helpful to look at more years following the policing changes after 2022, we can already see a pattern: even with law and enforcement changes, the problem does not disappear, it simply shifts location. The barriers on Capp Street may not have removed the issue, but instead moved it to other streets within the Mission District.

So to answer the question has the installation of barricades removed the sex workers from the streets or have they gone elsewhere to earn their money? We can only see the beginning of a pattern where the trade is pushed onto other streets in the neighborhood meaning that harder restrictions might not be the answer.
